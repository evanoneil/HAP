Executive Summary

When Hurricane Harvey hit the Texas Gulf Coast in August 2017, it was said to have not discriminated in who it effected. However, immigrants are impacted more severely and are slower to recover from natural disasters compared to those born in the United States. Reasons include potential language barriers, lack of social ties, and fears of drawing attention to their own or someone else’s legal status. With 25% of the Greater Houston region being comprised of foreign-born people, and the disproportionate impact of disasters on immigrants, particularly low-income immigrants, it is necessary that our disaster systems serve this vulnerable population.

The Houston Immigration Legal Services Collaborative offers the Humanitarian Action Plan, a coordinated disaster plan to serve all immigrants, to contribute to a more efficient and effective disaster response and recovery. The plan lays out recommendations for a collaborative effort to increase resilience of all immigrants through the next disaster to support the health and safety of all.

The twenty-nine recommendations within this plan are based on interviews, focus groups, a workshop and conversations with over eighty people from more than forty organizations and agencies. Informants include executive and legal directors of immigrant-serving organizations, case managers and social workers, undocumented immigrants, and representatives of systems-level providers and government agencies like the American Red Cross, United Way, and both City of Houston Office of Emergency and Harris County Office of Homeland Security and Emergency Management.

HILSC and our network of more than forty organizations have begun implementation of HILSC commitments and are committed to accomplishing each. Disaster plans must, however, exist within the local emergency response system. HILSC is also committed to supporting the success of systems-level providers with immigrant expertise and communication channels to their clients. In partnership with private and public agencies, HILSC is confident that policy and practices can be implemented to ensure a more effective and efficient response to the next disaster. Houston has named itself a Welcoming City. But this name is just a concept until there are actions and outcomes to make a tangible impact for close to a third of our population. The HILSC collaboration is ready for partnership in this challenge.

Three primary themes emerged:

1

Communication

The availability of accurate, timely and actionable information to immigrant populations will increase the efficiency of response and recovery, reduce rumors and help immigrants connect with resources.

2

Accessible resources

To increase resiliency, preparedness, response and recovery assistance must be available to all immigrants. Until publicly-funded resources are available they must be coordinated with private funding to ensure all disaster victims receive equitable aid.

3

Trauma-informed / culturally competent approaches

To ensure the resilience of all immigrants, it is essential that decision-makers and those who respond to disasters are familiar with how immigrants are particularly impacted by disasters and that those considerations are included in the planning, policy and implementation of preparedness, response and recovery efforts

HILSC appreciates feedback and to this end has created a webpage to track progress on all recommendations. Please share your successes as we progress together.
